Paper write-up cue: positioning vs Olmedo et al., RTAS 2020
============================================================

"Dissecting the CUDA Scheduling Hierarchy: a Performance and Predictability
Perspective" — Sañudo Olmedo, Capodieci, Martinez, Marongiu, Bertogna —
RTAS 2020. DOI 10.1109/RTAS48715.2020.00022.

This note is the citation-ready hand-off for the related-work / methodology
section of the FISH paper.  Companion technical detail lives in
notes/modeling_memset.txt; this file is just the surface arguments.


What Olmedo'20 actually delivers (verified by reading the paper)
----------------------------------------------------------------

  • Three-level GPU scheduling hierarchy (Section III, Fig. 4):
      1. Application scheduler — driver-level "channels" in a runlist,
         TDMA arbitration, timeslice + priority (low/medium/high).
      2. Stream scheduler — per-application; 2 discrete CUDA-stream
         priority levels since Maxwell.
      3. Thread-block scheduler + LRR warp scheduler.

  • Corrects the round-robin block-to-SM assumption that prior work
    used [4][18][19]:
      "we have shown that the policy adopted by the CUDA thread block
       scheduler to assign blocks to SMs may be different from the
       round-robin policy assumed in [4], [18], [19]"
    They derive the actual allocation rule.

      Eq. 2 (Maxwell/Pascal/Volta, mw=64): when an SM holds x warps,
      a y-warp block from a *different stream* lands on the same SM iff
          mw − x ≥ (⌊(mw−y)/y⌋ + 1) · y
      Eq. 3: generalization with z warps already on the SM (Turing).
      Eq. 4: load-balancing branch — drives SMs toward balance before
      reverting to RR.

  • Misprediction of an RR-only model reaches 96 % once multiple streams
    are used (Fig. 3, 7000 launch configs).

  • Reveals *hidden* inter-stream synchronisation: streams with different
    shared-memory configurations (cudaFuncSetCacheConfig) are forced to
    run sequentially even when streams would otherwise overlap.

  • Volta-and-later have separate FP32 + INT32 pipelines: WCET of a warp
    under a lockstep assumption is wrong; 16 FP vs 8 FP + 8 INT halves
    Volta total time, Pascal sees no benefit (Fig. 6).

  • Outside-the-SM contention (Section V):
      – memcpy interferes with kernel runtime up to ~3× (TX2);
      – memset interferes up to ~8× (TX2);
      – intra-GPC interference up to ~10× compared to inter-GPC.


Where Olmedo'20 stops, and FISH continues
-----------------------------------------

1. **GPU-only.**  The paper is strictly inside the device.  There is no
   ROS / middleware / OS-scheduler context anywhere in the analysis.
   Application-side callbacks are not mentioned because they are
   out-of-scope by design.  This is the largest single gap, and the one
   our table makes visible (rows: ROS 2 callbacks, callback↔kernel
   attribution, hierarchical OS→Exec→GPU).

2. **No callback-anchored task graph extraction.**  The paper measures
   on benchmark kernels written for the experiment (Mandelbrot, tiny
   vector adds).  There is no extraction from a real running
   application stack; the work is *characterisation*, not modelling-
   from-trace.  FISH provides the extraction layer.

3. **memset's hardware path is not characterised.**  Section V-A
   *quantifies* memset interference (Fig. 7 → ~8× on Pascal, similar on
   Volta) — but the paper does NOT decide whether memset runs on the
   Copy Engine or on the SMs.  In their task-set characterisation memset
   is bundled with "compute" without an explicit hardware-path
   commitment.

   This matters because in a Typed DAG task model the placement of
   memset (CE vs SM node) directly changes which scheduler bears the
   load in the RTA.  Treating memset as CE — the implicit assumption in
   many follow-up RT-GPU papers that cite Olmedo'20 — systematically
   underestimates SM contention.

   FISH commits to **memset = SM node** on Volta+, with empirical
   support in notes/modeling_memset.txt (yolo session: 11,195 memset
   rows on the same streams as kernel launches, stream-ordered, size
   regime where the driver routes through the SM fill kernel).  The
   paper write-up should:
      (a) cite Olmedo'20 for the interference *quantification*;
      (b) flag the *mechanism* as a gap they did not close;
      (c) point to FISH's commitment + supporting evidence as the
          closing contribution.


Citation-ready sentence (drop into Related Work)
------------------------------------------------

> "Olmedo et al. dissected the internal CUDA scheduling hierarchy on
>  NVIDIA platforms — runlist, stream FIFO, block-to-SM mapping, LRR
>  warp scheduler — and quantified up-to-10× inter-stream memory
>  interference [Olmedo'20].  FISH treats the hierarchy they derived as
>  its innermost layer and connects it upward to ROS 2 callbacks and
>  the OS executor.  In doing so we also close a residual gap in their
>  task-set characterisation: where Olmedo'20 leaves memset bundled
>  with "compute" without committing on its hardware path, FISH models
>  memset as an SM-side node (see Section ⟨X⟩), consistent with NVIDIA's
>  current driver behaviour on Volta and later."

Sub-sentence variant for the comparison table caption:

> "GPU scheduler ✓: Olmedo'20 dissects the on-device hierarchy.
>  FISH places that hierarchy as one layer inside a unified
>  ROS 2 + OS + GPU model."


Limitations Olmedo'20 themselves admit (useful for our defence)
---------------------------------------------------------------

  • "the complete block scheduling algorithm is not yet fully
     comprehended" — Eq. 4 holds for constant initial-warp count z′ only.
  • Register-pressure-dominated regime: "next to impossible" to evaluate
     experimentally.
  • Empirical tables are architecture-dependent; Turing changed mw from
     64 to 32 and required Eq. 3.  Future architectures will need
     re-verification.

These are honest, technical limitations.  Citing them — non-pejoratively
— supports our "we build on these foundations" framing.
